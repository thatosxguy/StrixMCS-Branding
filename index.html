00 — Premise

The Order

StrixMCS carries itself as an order — old, deliberate, selective. The strix, the watchful owl of augury, sits over an open book: vigilance above scholarship. The conceit is not decoration; it sets the standard the work is held to. Knowledge pursued, fellowship kept, and the steady ascent toward better things — for the maker and for those the work serves.

The Order is ancient in posture and current in execution. Its seal is old; its type is not. That contrast is the identity: a centuries-old emblem rendered in clean modern monospace reads as an old institution staffed by people who ship. Lean into the age; refuse the costume.

This is a personal and professional system, not a marketing brand. It exists so everything issued under the name — a public page, a private StrixHome config, a document, a dashboard — reads as one hand. Apply it the same whether or not anyone else will see the artifact. The Order keeps its standards in private.

01 — The Seal

One mark

The system has a single mark: the seal. The owl — sharp, angular, fierce — perched on the open book, in white line-art on absolute black. Nothing frames it: no laurel, no runes, no ground. The void is the frame. It is both the working mark and the regalia; one mark, used whole, at every scale that does it justice.

Use

  • Keep the seal intact — owl and book as one figure. Never crop or separate its parts.
  • Place only on absolute black. The white line-art needs the void to read; it is built for nothing else.
  • Give it size. The seal is regalia; do not shrink it to a cramped favicon. Where a tiny icon is unavoidable, the owl head alone may stand in — but the whole figure is always preferred.
  • No recolor, no rotation, no glow or bevel. The seal is finished as drawn.

Clear space

Keep clear space on all sides equal to the height of the owl head. Nothing — text, edge, or other element — intrudes into that margin. The seal is meant to sit alone, surrounded by the dark.

02 — Color

Three colors, held apart

The system is disciplined, not monochrome. White on black carries all text — crisp, cold, zero warmth. Oxblood is the structural accent: borders, rules, the mark of something that matters, never body text. Ceremonial gold is the rarest — reserved for moments of ceremony: the motto, a formal flourish, the front of a ceremonial document. Three colors, each with one job, never blurred together. No pastels; the palette is strong and dark by rule.

Discipline

  • White is for reading. All body and display text is white on black, with no warm tint. Pure, cold, legible.
  • Oxblood is structure, not language. It draws borders, underlines, and accents — it is never set as body copy, where its low contrast would fail.
  • Gold is ceremonial. It belongs to the motto and the front of formal things. The seal itself is white on black — gold is not part of the mark. Sprinkling gold into ordinary UI spends its weight; keep it rare.
  • No pastels, ever. Every color in the system is either near-black, crisp white, deep oxblood, or restrained gold. Nothing washed out.
03 — Typography

Mono leads

The voice of the Order is monospace — the type of the record, the credential, the config. IBM Plex Mono carries titles, labels, the wordmark, and the motto. A clean grotesque, IBM Plex Sans, carries running prose where mono would tire the eye. Modern faces, both — no nostalgia, no old-world serifs.

StrixMCS
The wordmark is written StrixMCS — always. Never STRIXMCS, never strixmcs, never Strix MCS. Capital S, then lowercase, then the acronym MCS in capitals. This casing is fixed in every context: prose, headers, code, signatures.
IBM Plex Mono
titles · labels · the wordmark · the motto · code
IBM Plex Sans — running text
For paragraphs and anything read at length. Regular weight, 15–16px, comfortable leading. Weight and size carry hierarchy; the system never reaches for bold as decoration.

Roles

  • Authority / display / labels: IBM Plex Mono → ui-monospace.
  • Running prose: IBM Plex Sans → Helvetica Neue → system-ui.
  • Headings carry normal case — the Order does not shout. All-capitals is reserved for nothing; even the wordmark and section titles use proper case.
04 — Naming Convention

The Strix names

Devices and instances under the Order take the Strix prefix. The convention is fixed: drop the space between words, and capitalize the first letter of the word that follows. The result is one closed compound, read as a single name.

NameStands for
StrixHomeThe Home Assistant instance — the apartment itself
StrixPhoneThe iPhone
Strixbook ProThe MacBook Pro
StrixBoxThe Xbox
StrixMCSThe entity itself — MCS in full capitals, as an acronym

Rule

  • Remove the separating space; capitalize the following word's first letter: Strix + Home → StrixHome.
  • Where the suffix is an acronym, set it in full capitals: StrixMCS, not Strixmcs.
  • The prefix is always Strix with a capital S and lowercase remainder — never STRIX, never strix.

06 — Voice

How it reads

Serious, precise, service-oriented. Documentation-minded. Plain verbs over jargon-for-its-own-sake. A dry, absurdist wit is allowed — but only where it never costs clarity.

Sounds like the Order

“StrixHome drives living-room lights from occupancy; bedtime mode overrides after 23:00.”

“Non-destructive by default. Review precedes implementation.”

“The technician visit is warranted — this is not a DIY signal problem.”

Not the voice

“Supercharge your smart home today!! 🚀”

“Leverage synergies across your IoT ecosystem at scale.”

“Oops! Something went wrong 😅”

07 — Application

One hand, everywhere

The system holds across artifacts that never sit side by side. A few fixed anchors make the consistency automatic — the same office issued the dashboard and the automation.

Screens & dashboards

  • Black field, panels in Near, white text. One element of emphasis per view.
  • Oxblood draws the structure — borders, active rules, the edge of the thing in focus.
  • Gold appears only where ceremony is earned: the seal on a title screen, the motto in a footer. The oxblood-and-gold Calibre dashboard is the sanctioned ceremonial register.
